Prospecting Playbook
B2B best practices baked into every signal, briefing, and outreach the engine generates
The Core Framework — Trigger, Window, Multi-Thread
Trigger-Based Only — Only reach out when something happened. A trigger creates urgency and gives you a natural, non-generic reason to reach out. No trigger = no signal worth pursuing.
The 90-Day Window — New executives have 90 days to prove themselves. They buy external advisory fastest in the first 60 days. An exec hire is your single best signal. Act within 1 week of the announcement.
Multi-Thread Always — Never rely on one contact. Identify the economic buyer (approves budget), the champion (uses the service), and the potential blocker (usually the CFO or legal). Reach all three independently.
Pain + Budget + Authority — A lead only converts when all three exist. No budget = waste of time. No authority = long sales cycle. No pain = no urgency. Score signals on all three, not just fit.
Outreach Rules That Actually Work
Pattern Interrupt — Your first sentence must break the pattern of every other email they get. Start with an insight, an observation, or a provocative question. Never: "I hope this finds you well." Never: "I came across your profile."
Give Before You Ask — Every first touch should include a gift: a relevant insight, a benchmark, a framework, a stat they haven't seen. You get a response by being useful, not by being clever.
One Ask Per Touch — Never ask for a meeting AND a response AND a call. One ask. The lower the friction, the higher the response rate. "Worth a conversation?" beats "Can we schedule 30 minutes?"
LinkedIn DM Under 150 Words — People read DMs on mobile. Every sentence must earn its place. If you can't say it in 150 words, you don't know what you're selling yet.
Follow-Ups Add Value — Never say "following up" or "circling back." Every follow-up touch adds a new piece of value — a case study, an insight, a relevant article. Earn the next reply.
The Challenger Sale — How to Frame Your Pitch
The highest-converting B2B sales approach is not to reflect the client's problem back to them — it is to teach them something about their problem they didn't already know. Reframe their situation. Show them a gap they can't see from inside.
Teach: Share an insight about their industry or situation that surprises them. "Companies that hire a new CDO without an external audit in the first 60 days typically spend 18 months undoing the first roadmap they built."
Tailor: Connect that insight to their specific trigger. Make it feel like you built this observation specifically for them.
Take Control: Recommend the entry point confidently. Don't ask what they want — tell them what they need and why. Confidence closes.
Entry Point Strategy — Start Small, Expand Fast
Never lead with your largest retainer. Lead with the lowest-risk, fastest-value engagement and let the work sell the expansion.
Executive Workshop ($18-30k) — Best cold entry. 1-2 days, fixed scope, clear deliverable. Easy to approve. Creates dependency for the roadmap.
Discovery Sprint ($15-20k) — Best for companies mid-transition. 30-45 days, audit + roadmap + investment case. Sells itself into a retainer.
Retainer (any tier) — Best sold after a Sprint or Workshop proves the value. Never cold. Always earned.
Signals That Predict Fast Closes
New executive + hiring surge together — New leader building their team. Needs strategic direction before the team runs in 10 directions. 30-day close window.
Bad quarter + board pressure — Budget unlocked by pain. Decision maker needs to show action fast. Fastest close cycle in B2B.
PE-backed + acquisition — PE partner is the real buyer. They want 90-day proof points. Entry via the operating partner relationship if possible.
Funding + DTC/digital pivot — Budget to spend, direction unclear, no internal expertise. Easiest Discovery Sprint pitch.
What to Avoid
Companies mid-layoff — No budget, survival mode. Wait 6 months.
Major consulting firm already engaged — If Deloitte, McKinsey, or Accenture is on record, the decision is made. Don't compete — wait for the contract to end.
No identifiable decision maker — If you can't find who owns digital/commercial strategy, the org is too opaque to sell into cold.
Companies in legal or regulatory trouble — Budget goes to lawyers. Not the time.
